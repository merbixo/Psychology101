Module 1: Introduction to Psychology and Its History

What is Psychology?

Psychology is formally defined as the scientific study of behavior and mental processes. We explore what people do (behavior) and their internal thoughts, feelings, perceptions, and memories (mental processes).

Goals of Psychology

Psychologists strive to:

  • Describe: What is happening?
  • Explain: Why is it happening?
  • Predict: When will it happen again?
  • Control/Change: How can it be modified?

A Brief History

While questions about the mind date back to ancient philosophers, psychology as a formal science began in the late 19th century.

  • Structuralism (Wundt, Titchener): Focused on breaking down mental experiences into basic elements using introspection. Established the first psychology lab in Leipzig, Germany (1879).
  • Functionalism (William James): Focused on how the mind allows people to adapt, live, work, and play. Influenced by Darwin's ideas.
  • Psychoanalysis (Sigmund Freud): Emphasized the role of unconscious conflicts, early childhood experiences, and repressed desires in shaping personality and behavior.
  • Behaviorism (Pavlov, Watson, Skinner): Focused only on observable behavior and how it's learned through conditioning (ignoring mental processes). Dominated early-mid 20th century.
  • Humanistic Psychology (Maslow, Rogers): Emphasized human potential, free will, and self-actualization. A reaction against behaviorism and psychoanalysis.
  • Cognitive Revolution (1960s): Renewed focus on mental processes like memory, perception, thinking, language, and problem-solving, aided by the advent of computers.

Modern Perspectives

Today, psychology integrates multiple perspectives:

  • Psychodynamic: Modern version of psychoanalysis, still focuses on unconscious and early experiences.
  • Behavioral: Focus on learning through rewards, punishments, and observation.
  • Humanistic: Focus on personal growth and fulfillment.
  • Cognitive: Focus on how we encode, process, store, and retrieve information.
  • Biological/Neuroscience: Focus on how the body and brain enable emotions, memories, and sensory experiences.
  • Evolutionary: Focus on how natural selection has shaped behavior and mental processes.
  • Sociocultural: Focus on how behavior and thinking vary across situations and cultures.

Key Takeaways

  • Psychology is the scientific study of behavior and mental processes.
  • It has evolved through various schools of thought, from structuralism to modern integrated perspectives.
  • Understanding these perspectives provides a framework for exploring different aspects of human experience.
